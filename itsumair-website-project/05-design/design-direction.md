# Design Direction

## The design problem to solve
This site must feel **premium enough to justify premium pricing** while remaining **plain enough for a 55-year-old contractor reading on a phone in Florida daylight**. Those pull in opposite directions. When they conflict, clarity wins.

## Design principles

1. **Clarity over cleverness.** Every design decision must survive the question: does this help a busy contractor understand faster?
2. **Proof is the hero.** Screenshots, numbers, before/afters get the most visual weight. Not decoration.
3. **Confident restraint.** Premium in this context reads as considered and uncluttered, not maximalist or trendy.
4. **High contrast always.** Accessibility and outdoor phone readability are conversion requirements.
5. **The face matters.** A real person's photograph is a design element, used deliberately and repeatedly.

## Visual character
- Clean, structured, generous whitespace
- Strong typographic hierarchy doing most of the work
- #6700C8 used decisively in a few places rather than sprinkled everywhere
- Full-bleed brand-color sections to break up long pages and anchor CTAs
- Cards with clear boundaries — contractors scan, boundaries help scanning
- Data and metrics styled distinctly so proof reads differently from marketing copy

## What to avoid
| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| Low-contrast grey-on-grey | Fails accessibility and fails outdoors on a phone |
| Thin font weights | Illegible for the target age range |
| Trendy oversized hero type with no substance | Reads as style over competence |
| Stock photos of people in suits shaking hands | Instantly reads as generic agency |
| Generic city skyline photos on location pages | Signals template, not local knowledge |
| Animation-heavy scroll effects | Slows the site, distracts, hurts Core Web Vitals |
| Dark mode as the default | Wrong for this audience and context |
| Carousels for important content | Poor engagement, accessibility problems |

## Emotional target
The visitor should feel: *this person is serious, he understands my business, and he is not going to waste my time.*

## Reference approach
Look at how premium trade suppliers and specialist B2B consultancies present, not at how design agencies present. The audience is not impressed by design for its own sake; they are reassured by competence signals.

## Mobile-first requirement
Design mobile first, genuinely. Most of this audience will first see the site on a phone, often from a link in an email or DM. Desktop is the secondary experience.
